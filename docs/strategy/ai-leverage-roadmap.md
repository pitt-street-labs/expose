# EXPOSE — Novel AI-Leverage Roadmap

**Status:** Advisory — not locked. Open for revision in subsequent sessions.
**Date:** 2026-05-09
**Author context:** AI-assisted synthesis from the locked spec-phase artifacts (SPEC.md, all 10 ADRs, positioning.md, ETHICS.md, framework-annotation.md), the prior Session-B competitive analysis, and current AI-security tooling research as of May 2026. Produced as Session D output to extend the three commercial AI-leverage ideas already on the books in ADR-009 with additional capabilities that strengthen EXPOSE's competitive moat.
**Public name:** EXPOSE (selected 2026-05-10 in Session H) / **Internal codename:** FF6K

This document identifies eleven additional AI-leverage capabilities organized into five themes that, taken together, deepen the structural moat described in `docs/positioning.md` while preserving the two-environment separation that anchors the safety story. It is **explicitly out-of-scope** for this document to propose features that violate the two-environment design (Environment 1 never calls Environment 2 capabilities; Environment 1 produces structured input only), to propose narrative-generation in E1 (per ETHICS.md non-goals), or to pre-empt Identity Surface module work (Session C; personnel-tracking AI is out of Core's scope).

This is advisory analysis. Treat it as input to subsequent roadmap, eval-harness, and module-specification work, not as a foundation document on the level of `positioning.md` or the ADRs.

## 1. Framing — what AI leverage means inside the two-environment design

ADR-009 enumerated three commercial AI-leverage ideas already on the books: APT targeting (Threat Context module), historical point-in-time enrichment (Threat Context module), and personnel-graph reconnaissance (Identity Surface module). Those are commercial-value plays — they extend Core's surface area into new ethics-bounded territory. They are not the only AI leverage available to the project.

The eleven capabilities in this document are different in kind. They strengthen Core's existing surface — attribution quality, sanitization defense, evaluation discipline — and they enrich the artifact contract that Environment 2 consumes downstream. They are organized to preserve a single architectural invariant:

> **Environment 1 produces structured, schema-validated, signed input. Environment 2 reasons over that input under appropriate safeguards. Environment 1 never depends on Environment 2; Environment 2 reasons over a contract Environment 1 publishes.**

Every capability in this document falls into one of two categories with respect to that invariant:

- **Environment 1 enhancements** (Themes A, B, C): capabilities that strengthen the deterministic engine itself. Bounded LLM use only — structured-output, schema-validated, no general tool access during enrichment, no narrative generation, all calls flow through `SafeLLMClient` (per ADR-005 / SPEC.md §8.4).
- **Environment 2 enablement** (Theme D): structured artifact extensions that make E2 consumption higher-quality, faster, and more auditable. Produced by Environment 1 from deterministic and bounded-LLM evidence; consumed by Environment 2 according to the operator's safety controls. Environment 1 emits scaffolds; Environment 2 fills the prose.
- **Long-horizon research candidates** (Theme E): capabilities that may justify investment in future phases but require deliberate ethical and architectural review before becoming roadmap items.

The current AI-security landscape (May 2026) is what makes these capabilities both possible and necessary. Multi-turn jailbreaks, multimodal injections, and MCP-server tool poisoning have matured into working attack patterns against frontier models; LiteLLM's PyPI compromise and the 1M+ exposed agent platforms scanned in early 2026 demonstrate that the AI infrastructure layer itself is now an attack surface. EXPOSE's bounded-LLM posture is genuinely defensible against this landscape, but only if the supporting evaluation, sanitization, and feedback loops are built deliberately. The capabilities in Themes B and C are exactly the ones the rest of the industry is groping toward; doing them deliberately and early is a moat.

## 3. Theme A — Attribution-quality enhancement (Environment 1)

The four Theme A capabilities tighten the central deliverable: defensible per-asset attribution with confidence tier and provenance chain. Every one strengthens what already exists in SPEC.md §8 rather than adding new surface area.

| ID | Capability | Motivation | Approach sketch | Ethics surface | Prerequisites | Success metric | Investment | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A1 | Confidence-calibration learning loop | Per-rule confidence weights are currently set by rule pack authors; analyst feedback on attribution decisions is not closed back into weight adjustment. Calibration drifts as the threat landscape and asset-attribution norms evolve. | Track per-rule precision/recall against analyst-flagged outcomes; compute Bayesian-updated confidence weight suggestions; surface to rule pack author as proposed deltas (never auto-applied to a tenant's live rule pack). Maintains determinism — at any commit the rule pack defines confidence, not learned weights. | Low — operates on attribution metadata only, not personal data. The "risk that calibration silently drifts" is mitigated by explicit human-in-loop on weight updates. | None (can ship in Phase 2). | Calibration-quality metric: ratio of `analyst_agrees` to `analyst_overrides` per rule per quarter, with non-decreasing trend after first calibration cycle. | 2-3 sprint-weeks for instrumentation + dashboard; ongoing effort for rule-pack-author cycle. | NIST AI RMF MEASURE 2.5 (TEVV), MEASURE 2.11 (Explainability). |
| A2 | Embedding-based asset clustering for novel-pattern detection | Rule-based attribution catches what its rules predict. Novel attribution patterns (a new cloud account naming convention, a previously-unseen subdomain pattern correlating with an existing org, a rare cert issuer used by one operator's infrastructure) are detected ad hoc by analysts. An embedding-space clustering pass over the observation graph surfaces unusual proximities that suggest novel rules to write. | Compute embeddings for `Subdomain`/`Service`/`Certificate` entities using deterministic feature vectors (cert issuer, port set, banner shape, CIDR proximity, registrant signal) plus a lightweight LM-assisted text feature for free-text fields. Cluster with HDBSCAN (no clusters-K hyperparameter required). Surface clusters to analysts as candidate rule-pack patterns; analyst decides whether to encode. Embedding model is a small, deterministic transformer (e.g., `all-MiniLM-L6-v2` class) running locally — no frontier LLM call per asset. | Low — uses public observation features only; does not enrich with private data. | A1 (calibration loop demonstrates feedback path works before scaling). | Number of analyst-confirmed novel rules per quarter sourced from clustering surface. Target: ≥1 per quarter after first six months. | 4-6 sprint-weeks for embedding pipeline; deterministic features dominate. | NIST AI RMF MAP 2.2 (Categorization), MEASURE 2.9 (Fairness/Bias) — clustering can amplify biases in feature engineering; explicit periodic review required. |
| A3 | LLM-assisted cohabitation-vs-cotenancy disambiguation | Reverse-IP and shared-cert observations produce false positives when many tenants legitimately share infrastructure (CDNs, shared hosting, multi-tenant SaaS, cloud regional load balancers). Today's rules apply blanket negative confidence weights to known shared-infrastructure patterns; the LLM enrichment pass can do better case-by-case judgment by reasoning over the structured observation set. | Augment SPEC.md §8.1 enrichment job: for `medium`-tier candidates whose only positive evidence is a `cohabits_ip_with` or shared-cert SAN edge, the LLM is asked a strictly bounded question — "given the structured observation set below, classify the cohabitation relationship as: same-tenant / shared-CDN / shared-hosting / multi-tenant-SaaS / unknown." Output is a strict enum plus a one-sentence rationale; both feed back into rule pack confidence aggregation. | Low — reasons over already-collected structured observations, no new collection. | None (can ship in Phase 2 alongside SPEC.md §8.1 jobs). | Reduction in analyst-overridden `cohabits_ip_with`-derived `medium` decisions per quarter. Target: ≥30% reduction within two quarters. | 2-3 sprint-weeks; primarily a new structured-output prompt + schema. | NIST AI RMF MEASURE 2.6 (Safety), MEASURE 2.11 (Explainability) — LLM rationale recorded in artifact. |
| A4 | Graph-based attribution path explanation generator | `Attribution.reasoning` today is a templated string referencing the rule(s) that fired. Analysts ingesting a `requires_review` candidate often ask "why did this end up here, what is the actual evidence chain?" The graph already has the answer; generating a structured, traversable explanation as part of the artifact saves analyst time and supports defensibility in client review. | Deterministic graph-traversal step at artifact-generation time (Stage 5) walks the rule-firing chain and the supporting graph edges; produces a structured `AttributionPath` object embedded in each `Attribution.reasoning` field. Each step references rule-ID, observation-ID, and edge-ID. **No LLM in this capability** — pure deterministic traversal. (LLM-assisted natural-language rendering is left to Environment 2 per the boundary.) | None — purely structural metadata. | None (can ship in Phase 2). | Analyst-reported time-to-comprehension on `requires_review` cases; aim for measurable reduction once shipped. | 1-2 sprint-weeks; primarily a new artifact section + traversal logic. | NIST AI RMF MEASURE 2.11 (Explainability) — even though no AI is used in producing the explanation, the explanation's existence is the substrate Environment 2's AI consumes. |

## 4. Theme B — Sanitization defense-in-depth (Environment 1)

SPEC.md §7 establishes the sanitization layer as a security property, not just code quality. The May 2026 prompt-injection-defense literature (PromptArmor, layered-defense work, the 73.2% → 8.7% attack-success-rate reduction reported with multi-layer defenses) consistently finds that **no single defense suffices**. Theme B adds three independent defenses to the existing sanitization layer; the union of defenses is the property worth building toward.

| ID | Capability | Motivation | Approach sketch | Ethics surface | Prerequisites | Success metric | Investment | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| B1 | Adversarial-content prompt-injection classifier | The `<external_observation>` tag pattern (SPEC.md §7.3) is the first line of defense. A second line — a dedicated classifier scoring each external string field for injection-likelihood — catches payloads that survived sanitization or that the tag-treatment alone is insufficient against. Real-world cert SAN injection attempts gathered during EXPOSE operation become the ongoing training set. | Small classifier model (DeBERTa-v3-base or comparable) fine-tuned on a labeled corpus of cert SAN, banner, DNS TXT, and WHOIS-org content with synthetic and real injection examples. Runs as a sanitization sub-stage; suspicious content is flagged on the entity (`content_flagged: injection_classifier:high`) and excluded from LLM prompt construction. Classifier outputs a probability, not a hard reject — operators tune the threshold per tenant. | Low — operates on already-collected public content. The corpus building requires ethical handling of any "real attack examples" — must be from operator-authorized targets only. | A1 (analyst feedback loop becomes the labeling pipeline). | False-positive rate <2% on benign cert SAN corpus; true-positive rate >85% on held-out injection corpus; quarterly re-evaluation per SPEC.md §11.2 eval-harness procedure. | 4-6 sprint-weeks for initial classifier; ongoing curation effort for the labeled corpus. | NIST AI RMF MEASURE 2.6 (Safety) — direct extension of the existing adversarial-injection eval dataset; MAP 3.4 (Mapping AI Risks) — sanitization-layer risk model extended. |
| B2 | Multi-LLM cross-validation for high-stakes attribution decisions | Tie-breaker escalation (SPEC.md §8.4) today is configurable but off by default. For attribution decisions where the cost of being wrong is highest — `requires_review` candidates that an analyst is likely to escalate, candidates whose attribution would gate Tier 3 active probing, candidates entering the artifact at `confirmed` tier — running the same prompt against two distinct providers and only emitting the attribution when both agree raises the quality floor materially. The cost model is bounded: this only fires on a small fraction of candidates. | Extend `SafeLLMClient` tie-breaker escalation: configurable "high-stakes attribution" predicate triggers cross-validation. Two distinct providers (e.g., Anthropic + OpenAI, or Anthropic + Gemini) are queried with the same prompt; outputs must match on the schema's primary decision field. Disagreement is logged and the attribution falls back to the rule-engine result with `cross_validation_disagreement: true` flag. Per-run cost ceiling still applies. | Low — reasons over already-collected structured observations. The "two providers can collude" risk is essentially zero given they are independent commercial APIs; the more serious risk is correlated bias across providers, which the eval harness should periodically test. | A3 (the high-value LLM enrichment jobs to cross-validate must exist). | Cross-validation agreement rate per attribution decision type per quarter; analyst-flagged false-positive rate on cross-validated decisions. | 2 sprint-weeks for the orchestration extension; ongoing cost-per-run-budget allocation. | NIST AI RMF MEASURE 2.7 (Security), MANAGE 1.1 (Risk Tolerance Operationalized) — explicit operationalization of cross-vendor disagreement as a risk signal. |
| B3 | Schema-violation root-cause analysis for prompt engineering iteration | When LLM outputs fail schema validation (per SPEC.md §8.4), the current behavior is to retry up to 2x and then escalate. The retry pattern hides systematic prompt-engineering issues — a particular prompt template silently failing 30% of the time is invisible until someone reads the audit log carefully. A structured root-cause-analysis pass over schema-violation logs (which patterns of input correlate with which patterns of failure) closes the loop on prompt iteration. | Periodic batch job over the audit log clusters schema-violation events by (prompt-template-ID, input-feature-shape, failure-mode). Output is a structured report surfaced to rule-pack and prompt maintainers. **No LLM in this capability** — pure log analysis with deterministic clustering. (Optional: later enhancement could ask an LLM to suggest prompt-template revisions, but kept manual for v1.) | None — operates on internal telemetry. | None (can ship alongside SPEC.md §8.4 audit logging). | Reduction in schema-violation rate per prompt template per quarter after each prompt-engineering iteration; trend should monotonically improve over the first year. | 2-3 sprint-weeks for the analysis job + reporting surface. | NIST AI RMF MANAGE 2.3 (Continuous Monitoring), MEASURE 2.5 (TEVV). |

## 5. Theme C — Eval and feedback (Environment 1, ongoing)

SPEC.md §11.2 commits to a Phase 2 eval harness with four held-out datasets (`confirmed_yours`, `confirmed_not_yours`, `ambiguous_with_resolution`, `adversarial_injection`) and quarterly re-evaluation. Theme C builds three operational capabilities on top of that commitment so the eval harness functions as living infrastructure, not a periodic ritual.

| ID | Capability | Motivation | Approach sketch | Ethics surface | Prerequisites | Success metric | Investment | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | Automated eval-dataset enrichment from analyst-flagged cases | Eval datasets curated only at quarterly intervals lag the threat landscape. Analyst-flagged cases (overrides on attribution decisions, escalations on tier transitions, manual additions of out-of-scope observations) are an ongoing source of high-quality eval data that today is not systematically captured. | Whenever an analyst overrides or escalates an attribution decision, the underlying observation set, the rule firing set, the LLM enrichment outputs (if any), and the analyst's labeled outcome are captured into a `candidate_eval_record` queue. Periodic curation pass reviews queued records for inclusion in the relevant held-out dataset. Includes deduplication, PII-stripping (per ADR-008), and tenant-context-stripping (cross-tenant leakage protection). | Medium — captures analyst decisions, which can include sensitive context. Tenant-isolation enforcement at curation step is critical. | A1 (the analyst-feedback channel must exist). | Eval-dataset growth rate per category per quarter; ratio of analyst-sourced vs. synthetic examples (target: ≥30% analyst-sourced after first year). | 3-4 sprint-weeks for the capture + curation pipeline; ongoing curation effort. | NIST AI RMF MEASURE 2.5 (TEVV), MAP 4.1 (Impact Assessment). |
| C2 | Cost-quality Pareto frontier dashboards per LLM provider | ADR-005 commits to multi-provider abstraction; SPEC.md §8.4 enforces per-call audit logging. The data exists to compute per-provider per-job-type cost-quality tradeoffs. Today nothing surfaces them. A Grafana dashboard plotting per-provider cost-per-attribution-decision against accuracy-on-eval-set lets operators make defensible provider-selection decisions. | OpenTelemetry-driven dashboard rendering: per provider × per job type × per quarter, plot (cost-per-call, accuracy-on-eval) as a scatter; surface the Pareto frontier. Operators can use this to choose primary vs. tie-breaker providers, to justify cost ceiling adjustments, and to detect quality regressions when a provider updates its model. | None — operates on internal telemetry. | C1 (eval growth must keep accuracy measurements current) and SPEC.md §8.4 audit logging (already committed). | Operator-decision time on provider selection; documented provider-change decisions referencing the dashboard as evidence. | 2-3 sprint-weeks for dashboard + queries; one-time effort. | NIST AI RMF MANAGE 1.1 (Risk Tolerance Operationalized), MANAGE 4.1 (Decommissioning) — provider-change decisions are documented. |
| C3 | LLM regression detection (model-version drift alerts) | When a frontier provider updates the underlying model behind a stable model-name handle (an event observed multiple times across providers in 2025-2026 per the model-drift literature), behavior on EXPOSE prompts can shift silently. Without detection, attribution accuracy can drift downward without anyone noticing until the next quarterly re-evaluation. | Daily lightweight regression test: a small fixed set of "canary prompts" (drawn from the eval datasets) is run against each configured provider; outputs are diffed against a recorded baseline. Significant divergence triggers an alert routed to the on-call rotation. Provider's published model-version changelog is also monitored where available. | None — operates on synthetic / curated test prompts only. | C1 (eval datasets provide the canary prompt source). | Mean time to detection of model-behavior shift; target ≤24 hours from provider update to alert. | 2 sprint-weeks for the harness; minimal ongoing maintenance. | NIST AI RMF MANAGE 2.3 (Continuous Monitoring), MEASURE 3.1 (Performance), MAP 4.1 (Impact Assessment). |

## 6. Theme D — Environment 2 enablement (artifacts feeding E2)

This is the moat-deepening theme. The current artifact (per SPEC.md §9 and `schemas/canonical-artifact-v1.json`) gives Environment 2 structured evidence; Theme D adds three structured scaffolds that make Environment 2 consumption materially higher-quality without violating the boundary. Each capability adds a new artifact-resident structure produced by Environment 1 from deterministic and bounded-LLM evidence; Environment 2 consumes that structure under whatever safeguards the operator's E2 program requires.

The integrity guarantee carried by these structures is the same as the rest of the artifact — cosign signature, full provenance, deterministic generation. Environment 2 verifies the signature once and trusts the contents according to its own policy; Environment 1 carries no opinion about Environment 2's reasoning.

| ID | Capability | Motivation | Approach sketch | Ethics surface | Prerequisites | Success metric | Investment | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| D1 | Structured "analyst question" prompts in artifact | Environment 2 analysts (whether using Mythos-class capabilities, Project Glasswing, or operator-administered tools) currently must compose their own prompts from scratch when reasoning about a target. Pre-formatting **structured questions** with provenance attached as part of the artifact reduces analyst friction, standardizes how E2 reasons about EXPOSE outputs, and makes E2 audit easier (a bounded set of questions, a bounded set of provenance-attached evidence). The questions are generated deterministically from the evidence shape, not by the LLM — a target with a `cohabits_ip_with` edge gets the cohabitation-question template; a target with `requires_review` tier gets the review-question template; etc. | Each candidate target in the artifact carries an `analyst_questions[]` field. Each question is a structured object: `question_template_id`, `provenance_evidence_refs[]`, `suggested_response_schema`, `confidence_context`. Question templates are versioned data, not generated text. Environment 2 consumes the structured questions and applies its own reasoning. | None — all questions reference observations already in the artifact; no new evidence. | A4 (the attribution-path structure provides the provenance refs). | Environment 2 operator-reported reduction in analyst time to first useful E2 output per target; analyst feedback on question template quality. | 3-4 sprint-weeks for the question-template engine + schema; ongoing template curation. | NIST AI RMF MAP 1.1 (System Context) — explicitly maps how Environment 2 consumes Environment 1 outputs. |
| D2 | "Adversarial perspective summaries" per target | A specific structured artifact field that lists, for each target, the structured evidence an attacker prioritizing this target would reason about: the unique/distinguishing observations (rare cert issuer, unusual port set), the linkage observations to other operator targets (lateral-movement adjacencies), the public observability characteristics (high vs. low signal in CT logs / passive DNS / scan datasets). **Generated deterministically from the observation graph**, not by an LLM — the LLM does not adopt an adversarial perspective in Environment 1. The structured summary is the substrate Environment 2 reasons over with whatever adversarial reasoning its safety controls permit. The clean separation matters: Environment 1 surfaces evidence; Environment 2 reasons about adversaries. | Per-target enrichment step at artifact-generation time computes `adversarial_perspective_summary`: structured lists of (a) `distinguishing_observations` (entities and edges with low population frequency in the observation graph), (b) `lateral_adjacencies` (graph paths to other operator-attributed targets within ≤2 hops), (c) `observability_characteristics` (per-collector visibility metrics for the target). | Higher than other Theme D capabilities — explicitly framing evidence in adversarial terms can be uncomfortable. The mitigations: (a) operator opt-in per tenant; (b) the structured form is evidence-only, no narrative; (c) inclusion in the artifact does not change EXPOSE's behavior at all — only Environment 2 consumption changes. The framing matches the existing dual-audience design (defensive CTEM operators and authorized red team operators alike benefit from the same structured perspective). | A2 (clustering provides the population-frequency baseline for distinguishing observations) and A4 (provenance for the structured fields). | Environment 2 operator-reported usefulness; per-tenant configuration adoption rate. | 4-5 sprint-weeks for the deterministic enrichment + schema; ongoing. | NIST AI RMF GOVERN 1.2 (Risk Tolerance Documented) — adversarial-framing is an explicit governance decision per tenant; MAP 3.4 (Mapping AI Risks). |
| D3 | Synthesizable narrative scaffolds (E2 fills in the prose; E1 supplies the structured hooks) | Environment 2 narrative work — red team briefings, board reports, CTEM lead summaries — currently re-derives structure each time. Standard narrative scaffolds (per-target lead briefing, per-cluster strategic summary, per-delta change report) with structured hooks pre-populated from the artifact let Environment 2 consumers produce consistent narrative output without re-deriving structure. The scaffolds are templates with structured slots (target-id refs, evidence-ref slots, attribution-confidence slots) — **no narrative text in Environment 1**. Environment 2's LLM tooling fills the slots; the scaffolds make E2 outputs comparable across runs and operators. | Artifact contains a `narrative_scaffolds[]` section with versioned scaffold templates, each scaffold referencing structured slots filled from the artifact's other sections. Templates are CC BY 4.0 (per ADR-009 EXPOSE Research alignment) — the scaffold catalog itself is published research. Environment 2's downstream tooling renders narrative from the scaffold. | None — scaffolds are structural templates; narrative remains in Environment 2. | D1 + D2 (the structured questions and adversarial-perspective summaries are the slot inputs). | Environment 2 operator adoption; consistency-of-output measurement across operators using the same scaffold. | 4-6 sprint-weeks for initial scaffold catalog + slot-population; ongoing catalog curation as a CC BY 4.0 publication stream. | NIST AI RMF MAP 1.1 (System Context), GOVERN 1.5 (Documentation) — scaffolds are documentation of the E1 → E2 contract. |

## 7. Theme E — Long-horizon possibilities (research, not roadmap-committed)

These are explicitly **R&D candidates**, not roadmap items. They are surfaced to make the moat depth claim defensible — there is a credible research trajectory beyond the eleven concrete capabilities in Themes A-D — but each requires deliberate ethical and architectural review before any implementation commitment. The 2026 federated-learning-for-cybersecurity literature shows the technique is mature enough to be considered; the cross-deployment privacy guarantees still need work specific to EXPOSE's threat model.

| ID | Capability | Why interesting | Why deferred |
|---|---|---|---|
| E1-cap | Federated learning across deployments (without sharing raw data) | Multiple EXPOSE deployments could collaborate on rule-pack improvement and adversarial-injection-pattern detection without sharing tenant data — only model updates / classifier weights / aggregated statistics flow between deployments. The literature (DFedForest++ and related 2026 frameworks for privacy-preserving cyber threat intelligence) demonstrates it is operationally feasible. | Tenant-isolation is a v1 first-class property (ADR-007); federated learning fundamentally adds a new cross-tenant cross-deployment data flow. The threat model for that flow requires careful design — adversaries spawning a federated learning peer to extract model-extraction signal from real deployments, federated-aggregation poisoning attacks, differential-privacy parameter selection. Customer expectations on data sovereignty (especially federal customers) rule out anything that could be misperceived as "sending data to Korlogos servers." Worth a dedicated session in 2027. |
| E2-cap | Generative attribution-rule discovery from analyst feedback | An LLM-assisted process that proposes new declarative rules (in the rule pack DSL) from accumulated analyst-feedback signals. Closes the loop from A1 (calibration) through A2 (clustering) to actual rule-pack evolution. Could materially accelerate rule-pack maturity. | Rule packs are deliberately data-not-code with a closed predicate vocabulary (SPEC.md §8.2). Generative rule discovery raises a meaningful supply-chain integrity question — generated rules need human review before merge, the LLM could subtly bias the rule pack toward higher false positives, and rule-pack ownership becomes ambiguous if rules are auto-suggested. A1 + A2 + C1 in production for at least one full year is the prerequisite for considering this seriously. |
| E3-cap | Self-supervised graph-completion for partial-observation deployments | Some deployments operate with restricted collector sets (cost-bound research deployments, federally-restricted deployments running only Tier 1 passive collectors, etc.). A graph-completion model trained on richer deployments could fill in likely-missing edges (predicted `resolves_to`, predicted `cohabits_ip_with`) with a confidence probability for partial-observation deployments. | Inference-on-missing-edges fundamentally changes the EXPOSE attribution contract — today every claim has an explicit observation; with predicted edges, claims would have predicted observations. The defensibility-of-attribution story (a moat per `positioning.md`) loses something here. Worth research, but only as an explicitly-flagged predicted-evidence track that does not contaminate the canonical observation graph. |

The eleven concrete capabilities (Themes A-D) are phaseable against the existing build plan in SPEC.md §11. Theme E remains uncommitted.

| Phase | Capability | Rationale for phase placement |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 2 (LLM enrichment, 4-6 weeks per SPEC.md §11.2) | A3 (cohabitation/cotenancy disambiguation) — slots into Sprint 11-12 enrichment jobs. B3 (schema-violation root-cause analysis) — slots into Sprint 13-14 eval harness. | Phase 2 is the natural home for the bounded LLM enrichment extensions; A4 is deterministic and can ship even earlier as a Phase 1 hardening task. |

C3 (LLM regression detection) — requires the eval-dataset infrastructure from Phase 2. D1 (structured analyst questions) — requires A4 from Phase 2. | These capabilities ship in production-hardening because they depend on production-grade telemetry and on accumulated evidence. |

The phasing respects the dependency graph. A4 unblocks D1; D1 unblocks D3; A1 unblocks B1, C1; A2 unblocks D2; etc. The dependency-respecting order is also the value-respecting order — each capability shipping at its earliest plausible point produces outputs that the next capability builds on.

## 9. Competitive differentiation — depth-of-moat per theme

For each theme, what would a competitor have to build to match? This is where the "moat" claim earns its keep. The competitive analysis (`docs/strategy/competitive-analysis.md` §11) already established that no commercial EASI vendor publishes a transparent LLM contract. This section deepens that — what would a vendor have to build per theme to neutralize EXPOSE's leverage?

| Theme | What a competitor must build | Plausibility | Time-to-match |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A. Attribution-quality enhancement** | Open attribution rule-pack format; analyst-feedback loop architecture; embedding pipeline; LLM enrichment job architecture; per-rule calibration framework. Existing commercial EASMs treat their attribution as a closed black box (Censys's "95% accuracy" is a number, not a methodology). Matching A1-A4 requires a fundamental architectural shift to operator-inspectable attribution. | Low for Mandiant, Censys, Defender EASM (closed-attribution architecture is a moat for them in the opposite direction); medium for SpiderFoot HX (open OSS heritage but no first-class attribution model); high for an open-core entrant willing to compete on architectural transparency. | 18-24 months for a vendor with an existing EASM platform; 9-12 months for a new entrant. |
| **B. Sanitization defense-in-depth** | Adversarial-injection eval dataset; classifier model + curation pipeline; multi-LLM cross-validation orchestration; prompt-engineering iteration discipline. The May 2026 prompt-injection landscape is moving fast enough that the depth-of-defense is genuinely a moving target — vendors who invest now establish a defensible position. | Medium for any vendor with a dedicated AI security team (Microsoft, CrowdStrike, Palo Alto); low for vendors treating LLM enrichment as a marketing feature without supporting infrastructure. | 12-18 months. The bottleneck is not classifier training; it is the curated corpus of real cert SAN, banner, DNS TXT injection examples — and that corpus is operator-derived. EXPOSE's federal deployment pathway (per ADR-010) gives unusual access to such corpora once federal customers start sharing examples. |
| **C. Eval and feedback** | Eval harness infrastructure; held-out dataset curation; cost-quality dashboard; regression detection harness. The architecture is straightforward; the discipline of running it quarterly and acting on the outputs is the moat. Most vendors do not publish their eval methodology. | Medium for any vendor; the difficulty is institutional, not technical. | 6-12 months for the infrastructure; 2+ years to build dataset depth. |
| **D. Environment 2 enablement** | Adoption of the two-environment model in the first place. Most commercial EASM vendors collapse Environment 1 and Environment 2 into a single platform — they have an LLM that does narrative reasoning over their own data. To match D1-D3 a vendor would have to (a) re-architect to separate the deterministic substrate from the narrative-reasoning layer, (b) accept the operational complexity of an explicit handoff, (c) commit to an artifact contract their narrative tooling consumes. The architectural shift is substantial. | Very low — the two-environment model is a deliberate architectural choice that imposes complexity for safety benefit. Vendors selling integrated SaaS will not voluntarily impose the complexity on themselves; the "all in one platform" pitch is too commercially compelling. | 24+ months and a major architectural rebuild. **This is the deepest moat in the document.** |
| **E. Long-horizon research candidates** | A research budget and a willingness to publish methodology under permissive license (CC BY 4.0 / Apache 2.0). EXPOSE Research (per ADR-009) is the structural mechanism. | Low for vendors whose business model depends on closed-IP data depth; high for vendors that already publish research (Microsoft Research, Google Research, Mandiant CTI publications). | Indefinite — research moats compound across years. |

The depth-of-moat is not uniform across themes. **Theme D is the deepest** because the architectural shift required to match it conflicts with the dominant commercial model in the EASM space. **Theme A is the broadest** because every competitor that wants to compete on attribution credibility must eventually build something resembling A1-A4. **Themes B, C, E** are domains where investment compounds, with depth growing in proportion to time-in-market.

## 11. Mythos integration considerations

The two-environment model anticipates Mythos-class downstream consumption (per ADR-005's "When to revisit" section and SPEC.md §12 open question on Mythos integration). EXPOSE Core does not require Mythos and does not depend on it; the artifact contract is the integration surface.

The Theme D capabilities are Mythos-aware-but-not-Mythos-dependent by design. D1's structured analyst questions, D2's adversarial perspective summaries, D3's narrative scaffolds are all formats that any Environment 2 LLM tooling can consume — Project Glasswing accessible Mythos, internal Korlogos red-team tooling, future GA frontier models, locally-hosted models. The artifact remains agnostic.

What changes if Environment 2 is Mythos-class specifically:

- **Question-template depth.** Mythos-class capabilities can reason over deeper structured-question chains (multi-step analyst questions where the answer to one informs the next). D1 should publish a question-template-depth indicator so Environment 2 consumers know when a question chain expects high-capability iterative reasoning vs. when a single-shot smaller model suffices.
- **Scaffold complexity.** D3's narrative scaffolds can carry per-scaffold complexity hints — some scaffolds are intended for high-capability narrative synthesis; others are intended for narrower bounded LLM workflows. Mythos-class operators consume the high-complexity scaffolds; smaller-LLM operators consume the bounded ones.
- **Adversarial-perspective depth.** D2's structured adversarial summaries can carry a "depth-of-reasoning" indicator: shallow (factual evidence only) vs. deep (graph-path adversarial reasoning over multi-hop adjacencies). Operators with appropriate Environment 2 safeguards consume the deep version; operators without consume only the shallow.

The integration surface is a structured contract on the artifact side. Coordination with Project Glasswing or any future Mythos GA happens at the contract boundary, not inside Environment 1. This preserves the property that EXPOSE Core is a deliverable independent of Mythos availability — federal customers self-hosting EXPOSE today consume the artifact with whatever Environment 2 tooling their authorization permits, and Mythos integration becomes an Environment 2 enhancement when those operators adopt it.

The deliberate anticipation in the artifact format (carrying complexity hints, depth indicators, scaffold tier labels) is the moat. A competitor that adds Mythos integration after the fact has to retroactively design the artifact contract; EXPOSE designs it in.

## 14. Closing — moat depth in one paragraph

The eleven concrete capabilities in Themes A through D, taken together, produce a structural moat that would require a competitor to (a) adopt an open attribution architecture (Theme A), (b) invest in multi-layer prompt-injection defenses with adversarial corpora derived from production deployment (Theme B), (c) build and operate a continuous evaluation and feedback discipline at production cadence (Theme C), and (d) most critically, accept the architectural shift to a two-environment model with structured contracts at the boundary (Theme D). No incumbent commercial EASM vendor delivers any of the four; the integrated-SaaS commercial model in particular conflicts directly with Theme D. EXPOSE's competitive position is not feature-incremental; it is structural, and these capabilities are how the structure compounds. The three R&D candidates in Theme E are surfaced to make the trajectory credible — there is more depth to come — but the immediate moat is in A through D, sequenced through the existing Phase 2 / Phase 3 / Phase 4 build plan.
